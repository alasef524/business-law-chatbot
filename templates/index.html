Contract Act, 1872 Click to view description

Law of Contract

The law of contract is regulated by the Contract Act, 1872.

Object of the Law of Contract

The law of contract is that branch of law which determines the circumstances in which promises made by the parties to a contract shall be legally binding on them.

In simple words, the purpose of the law of contract is to ensure the realization of reasonable expectations of the parties who enter into a contract.

According to Sir William Anson, an agreement, to become a contract, must give rise to a legal obligation or duty.

An obligation is a legal tie which imposes upon a definite person or persons the necessity of doing or abstaining from doing a definite act or acts.

Example: A agrees to sell his car to B for Rs. 1,00,000. The agreement gives rise to an obligation on the part of A to deliver the car to B and on the part of B to pay Rs. 1,00,000 to A. This is a legal agreement and therefore it is a contract.

An agreement which gives rise only to social obligation is not a contract.

Agreement and Its Types

An agreement is a wider term. An agreement may be a social agreement or a legal agreement.

If A invites B to dinner and B accepts the invitation, it is a social agreement. A social agreement does not give rise to contractual obligations and is not enforceable in a Court of law.

Only those agreements which are enforceable in a Court of law are contracts.

Example: A father promises to pay his son Rs. 100 every month as pocket allowance. Later he refuses to pay. The son cannot recover it because it is a domestic agreement and there is no intention to create legal relations.

Essentials of a Valid Contract: Section 2(h) and Section 10

  1. Offer and acceptance
  2. Intention to create legal relationship
  3. Lawful consideration
  4. Capacity of parties / competency
  5. Free and genuine consent
  6. Lawful object
  7. Agreement not declared void
  8. Certainty and possibility of performance
  9. Legal formalities

Sale of Goods Act, 1930

Goods

Goods include every kind of movable property except actionable claims and money.

Classification of Goods

Goods can be classified into existing goods, future goods, and contingent goods.

1. Existing Goods

Existing goods are goods which are already in existence and are physically present in some person's possession and ownership.

Existing goods may be specific and ascertained, meaning they can be identified as separate things, or generic and unascertained, meaning they are indicated by description and not separately identified.

2. Future Goods

Future goods are goods which will be manufactured, produced, or acquired by the seller after the making of the contract of sale.

Example: P agrees to sell to Q all the mangoes which will be produced in his garden next year. This is an agreement for the sale of future goods.

3. Contingent Goods

Contingent goods are goods whose acquisition by the seller depends upon a contingency which may or may not happen. They come within the class of future goods.

Example: X agrees to sell to Y a certain ring provided he is able to purchase it from its present owner. This is an agreement for the sale of contingent goods.

Sale and Agreement to Sell: Section 4

A contract for the sale of goods may be either a sale or an agreement to sell. Where ownership in the goods is transferred from the seller to the buyer, the contract is called a sale.

Where the transfer of ownership is to take place at a future time or subject to a condition to be fulfilled later, the contract is called an agreement to sell.

Differences Between Sale and Agreement to Sell

  1. In an agreement to sell, ownership remains with the seller until the agreed time expires or the agreed conditions are fulfilled. In a sale, ownership passes to the buyer immediately.
  2. In a sale, the goods belong to the buyer, and the buyer bears the loss if the goods are later damaged or destroyed. This follows Section 26.
  3. In a sale, the unpaid seller may have remedies such as lien, stoppage in transit, and resale. In an agreement to sell, the seller's remedy is generally a suit for damages.

Essential Elements of a Contract for Sale of Goods

  1. There must be a contract for the exchange of movable goods for money. Exchange of goods for goods is not a sale.
  2. The buyer and seller must be different persons because a person cannot buy from or sell to himself. However, a part-owner can sell to another part-owner.
  3. A contract of sale is made by an offer to buy or sell goods for a price and acceptance of that offer. This follows Section 5(1).
  4. A contract of sale may be in writing, by word of mouth, or implied from the conduct of the parties. This follows Section 5(2).
  5. The parties may agree on terms relating to time, place, and mode of delivery. Essential terms are called conditions and non-essential terms are called warranties.
  6. Price means the money consideration for the sale of goods. If no price is fixed, the buyer must pay a reasonable price. This follows Section 9.
  7. A contract for the sale of specific goods becomes void if the goods, without the knowledge of the seller, have already perished or become so damaged that they no longer answer their description. This follows Section 7.
  8. A contract for sale of goods must satisfy all essential elements of a valid contract, such as competency, free consent, consideration, and lawful object.

Negotiable Instruments Act, 1881

Meaning of Negotiable Instruments

Documents of a certain type used in commercial transactions and monetary dealings are called negotiable instruments.

"Negotiable" means transferable by delivery, and "instrument" means a written document by which a right is created in favour of some person. Therefore, negotiable instrument literally means a document transferable by delivery.

Section 13(1) states that a negotiable instrument means a promissory note, bill of exchange, or cheque payable either to order or to bearer.

Promissory Note: Section 4

A promissory note is an instrument in writing, not being a bank note or currency note, containing an unconditional undertaking signed by the maker to pay a certain sum of money only to, or to the order of, a certain person, or to the bearer of the instrument.

The person who promises to pay is called the maker. The person who will receive the money is called the payee.

Essential Elements of a Promissory Note

  1. The instrument must be in writing.
  2. It must contain an express promise to pay.
  3. The promise to pay must be unconditional.
  4. The maker must be certain and definite.
  5. The instrument must be signed by the maker.
  6. The sum of money to be paid must be certain.
  7. The money must be payable to a definite person or according to that person's order.
  8. The payment must be in legal tender money.

Example: "Mr. Sen I.O.U. Rs. 1000" is only an admission of debt. There is no promise to pay, so it is not a promissory note.

Bill of Exchange: Section 5

A bill of exchange is an instrument in writing containing an unconditional order, signed by the maker, directing a certain person to pay a certain sum of money only to, or to the order of, a certain person, or to the bearer of the instrument.

The maker of a bill of exchange is called the drawer. The person directed to pay is called the drawee. The person who receives the money is called the payee. After acceptance, the drawee becomes the acceptor.

Cheque: Section 6

A cheque is a bill of exchange drawn upon a specified banker and payable on demand.

A cheque may be payable to bearer or to order, but it must be payable on demand. The banker must pay it when presented during usual office hours, provided the cheque is validly drawn and the drawer has sufficient funds.

A cheque must be dated. A banker may refuse to pay an undated cheque. A cheque drawn with a future date is valid, but it is payable only on and after the date specified. Such cheques are called post-dated cheques.

If there is too much delay in presenting a cheque, the bank may consider it suspicious and refuse to honour it. A stale cheque is usually considered too old after six months in India and Bangladesh.

Partnership Act, 1932

Essential Elements of a Partnership: Section 4

Section 4 defines partnership as the relation between persons who have agreed to share the profits of a business carried on by all or any of them acting for all.

  1. There must be an agreement between two or more persons.
  2. The agreement must be to share the profits of a business.
  3. The business must be carried on by all or any of them acting for all.

The first element shows the voluntary contractual nature of partnership. A partnership can arise only from an agreement, express or implied. Where there is no agreement, there is no partnership.

Section 5 states that the relation of partnership arises from contract and not from status.

The second element shows that business is essential to partnership. Business includes trade, occupation, or profession.

The third element is the most important feature of partnership. Partners are both agents and principals. The business may be carried on by all or by any one or more of them on behalf of all.

Tests of a True Partnership: Section 6

Section 6 states that in determining whether a group of persons is a firm or whether a person is a partner, regard shall be given to the real relation between the parties as shown by all relevant facts taken together.

Sharing of profits is important but not conclusive. Profit sharing may exist even where there is no partnership.

  1. A creditor taking a share of profits in lieu of interest and part-payment of principal.
  2. An employee receiving a share of profits as remuneration.
  3. Workers receiving a share of profits as bonus.
  4. Widow or children of a deceased partner receiving a share of profits as annuity.

Partnership Forbidden by Law

  1. Section 11 of the Companies Act, 1994 prohibits formation of a partnership for banking business with more than ten persons and for any other purpose with more than twenty persons.
  2. An agreement to form a partnership for carrying on a trade prohibited by law is void.

Deed of Partnership

The agreement to carry on business in partnership may be oral or written. If written, the document containing the terms is called the Deed of Partnership or Articles of Partnership.

A partnership deed usually includes the names and addresses of partners, firm name, nature of business, place of business, duration, capital contribution, profit-sharing ratio, mode of management, powers of partners, retirement, expulsion, and admission of new partners.

Registration of Firms

Registration of a partnership is not compulsory. However, an unregistered firm suffers from certain disabilities, so registration is necessary for carrying on business effectively.

Registration may be done by sending or delivering to the Registrar of Firms a statement in the prescribed form with the prescribed fee.

  1. Firm name
  2. Principal place of business
  3. Other places where the firm carries on business
  4. Date when each partner joined the firm
  5. Full names and permanent addresses of partners
  6. Duration of the firm

Consequences of Non-Registration: Section 69

  1. A partner of an unregistered firm cannot file a suit against the firm or any partner to enforce a right arising from contract or the Partnership Act.
  2. No suit can be filed on behalf of an unregistered firm against a third party to enforce a right arising from contract.
  3. An unregistered firm cannot claim a set-off in a suit.

Exceptions

  1. A partner of an unregistered firm can sue for dissolution and accounts.
  2. Suits may be filed for realization of properties of a dissolved firm.
  3. The Official Assignee or Receiver can realize properties of an insolvent partner.
  4. There is no bar where registration provisions do not apply by government notification.
  5. An unregistered firm may file a small claim not exceeding Tk. 100 where allowed.
